# Claims and proof

Every marketing claim must be defensible — traceable to an approved feature, evidence item, or
messaging claim. This skill governs HOW to phrase claims so they survive the claim/check gate and
never become an unsupported assertion.

## Rules

- A claim is only as strong as its evidence. If you can't point to approved evidence or messaging, don't make the claim.
- Prefer specific, verifiable statements ("X now does Y") over vague superlatives ("the best", "revolutionary", "10x").
- Never invent metrics, percentages, customer names, dates, or roadmap promises — a number must come from approved evidence.
- Attribute outcomes to the capability, not to hype: "catches conformance gaps before a live buyer" beats "guarantees flawless checkout".

## Writing a claim

1. State the user-facing capability in plain language.
2. Tie it to the concrete change or evidence that makes it true.
3. Scope it honestly — say what it does, not what it might do.

## Fails the gate (avoid)

- A metric with no evidence ("reduces errors by 90%").
- A capability not present in the approved feature set.
- A comparative/competitor claim with no approved backing.
- An availability or date promise not in the release.

This complements `redaction-rules` (never expose secrets/PII) on the truthfulness axis: redaction
keeps you safe; claim-and-proof keeps you honest.
